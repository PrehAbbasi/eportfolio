Reflective Writing

Introduction

This reflective essay explores my personal learning journey through the big data technologies and management module. Adopting Schön’s reflective model (Gordon, 2025), I examine how I evolved from an initially overwhelmed learner into a confident practitioner competent in data governance, ETL, SQL querying, and relational schema design.

Initial Challenges and Adopting a Reflective Approach

At the outset of the module, I felt a mixture of excitement and anxiety. In a personal log, I noted “a lot of confusion regarding the e-portfolio and unit 6 assessment”. I was unsure how to organise my work on GitHub for the portfolio, and even the submission instructions. In the first week I jumped straight to assignment briefs without a clear study plan which felt like a rocky start and left me doubting my abilities and uncertainty about how to proceed.
To overcome this, I embraced reflective practice, treating uncertainty as a learning opportunity. I kept a weekly journal of what I understood and struggled with. After Week 1, I noted questions (e.g., e-portfolio content) and sought answers from the tutor and forums. Clarification helped reduce confusion.

In one instance, while trying to import a large dataset into a database, I noticed the process failing repeatedly. Rather than giving up, I paused to troubleshoot and adjust my approach (splitting the data into chunks). This on-the-spot adjustment not only solved the technical issue but also taught me the value of staying calm and analytical under pressure. By reflecting both during action and after, I gradually turned initial confusion into clarity. I also took proactive steps to organise myself, such as creating an assignment planner for Unit 6 to manage the group project timeline. Adopting this reflective mindset early on set the tone for my learning journey, enabling me to transform uncertainties into meaningful learning experience.

Developing Data Wrangling and ETL Skills

One of the first major themes of the module was data wrangling, cleaning, and transformation. I recall spending hours dealing with parsing errors and null values, which was frustrating at first. However, this challenge taught me perseverance and attention to detail. Through trial and error, I learned systematic data cleaning, removing duplicates, standardising formats, and validating transformations I now see data wrangling as a critical foundation for analysis, improving query performance. I have grown from fearing messy data to confidently valuing data wrangling as a core technical skill and mindset.

Mastering Database design and SQL Querying

Earlier in the module, I only had a basic understanding of relational databases, and designing a schema from scratch felt daunting. Concepts such as normalisation, primary keys, and ACID transactions seemed abstract. Through the coursework and project, these ideas became practical. I learned to convert real-world data requirements into an ER diagram and a relational schema. Studying normalisation was a key turning point. At first, I struggled with 1NF–3NF, but practice helped me normalise our project database to 3NF, removing anomalies and duplication, improving consistency. With indexing, complex queries ran faster with accurate results.
Alongside design, I significantly improved my SQL querying skills throughout the module. By the end, I confidently wrote multi-table JOINs, subqueries, and aggregate queries to answer analytical questions. For instance, I created one query to calculate total sales per region per quarter by joining four tables, which felt like a major achievement. Understanding DBMS transactions and ACID properties became essential during ETL loading. Overall, this progress strengthened both my technical capability and confidence.

APIs and Compliance

Modern big data work requires integrating diverse systems with strong governance. This module introduced APIs for data integration and emphasised governance and compliance. Initially, pulling data via APIs felt advanced and outside my comfort zone, so I quickly learnt APIs are protocols enabling software applications to exchange data. Learning APIs broadened my technical horizon, showing databases aren’t the only data source, live services stream data too. It taught me modular, error-tolerant coding for unpredictable network fetching which is vital in workplaces where API-based inter-system communication is ubiquitous.
Alongside technical skills, I developed a strong appreciation for data governance, security, and regulatory compliance. Initially, topics like GDPR and data policies felt abstract and less engaging than hands-on work. However, through our project’s data management discussions, I realised how essential governance is in any data environment. We explored governance frameworks and legal responsibilities for handling personal data, including data ownership, access controls, encryption, and anonymisation. In practice, I separately supported GDPR compliance by pseudonymising customer data for my own working, such as hashing customer IDs when identification was unnecessary. This reinforced the ethical duty to protect privacy and use data responsibly. Reflecting on my learning, I shifted from seeing compliance as a peripheral administrative task to recognising it as central to big data project success. Integrating APIs with governance matured my outlook, showing big data involves not only Volume, Variety, and Velocity, but also Veracity and valid use.

Collaborative Learning

Feedback from peers and tutors was vital to my development. Constructive guidance revealed blind spots and pushed my learning further. After an early SQL draft, my tutor said my query worked, but my explanation lacked depth because I did not justify my approach. This prompted me to revisit lecture material on query optimisation, strengthen my rationale, and reference indexing in the final submission. The experience taught me to be critical and analytical about technical decisions, not merely descriptive. I applied this in later units by justifying design choices, such as using a star schema and selecting appropriate data types.
Peer feedback was invaluable during Unit 6’s GreenMart group project. Virtual coordination was challenging, but regular review meetings helped. My group helped me to see final report in new light and we made some changes. These inputs enhanced our project and my skills. Collaboration strengthened my teamwork and communication. I learned to exchange feedback respectfully, manage tension, and value diverse views.

Conclusion

Reflecting on this module, I can clearly see major personal and professional growth. Professionally, I built a strong foundation in big data: data wrangling, normalised schemas, ETL pipelines, API integration, and data compliance, skills that felt unfamiliar months ago. Personally, I developed resilience and reflective learning. Early confusion and self-doubt shifted into competence and self-awareness as I learned to break complex topics into manageable steps and continuously improve. I became more organised through planning and journaling, strengthened communication through the e-portfolio and teamwork, and gained patience through late-night data cleaning and debugging. Overall, I feel better prepared academically and professionally.
