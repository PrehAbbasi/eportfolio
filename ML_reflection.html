Reflective Writing

Introduction and Context

Across this Machine Learning module I progressed from foundational concepts of Industry 4.0 and Industry 5.0 in Unit 1 to an independently evaluated comparison of a classical model and a deep learning model in Units 11 and 12. My e-portfolio documents an autoencoder based anomaly detection project delivered as a team in Unit 6, and a Spotify content-based ranking project in which I trained and compared a HistGradientBoostingRegressor against a Multilayer Perceptron. Using Gibbs' (1988) reflective cycle and the four stage model of context, description, analysis and evaluation, this piece considers what I learned, how feedback shaped my growth, my contributions to others' work, and the professional judgement I developed.

Description

Units 1 to 5 built foundational literacy in digital transformation, statistical reasoning, and unsupervised clustering, while Units 2, 4 and 10 required me to critically evaluate peers' discussions of the CrowdStrike outage and generative AI governance. Unit 6 asked my team to build and defend an autoencoder for self-supervised anomaly detection, justifying a 95th-percentile reconstruction error threshold. Units 7 to 9 covered neural network and computer vision architectures alongside their ethical implications. Units 11 and 12 culminated in an independent comparison of a classical gradient-boosting model and a deep neural network on the Spotify Tracks dataset (Pandya, no date), evaluated using RMSE, MAE, NDCG@10, GroupKFold validation, SHAP explainability and subgroup bias diagnostics.

Analysis

The clearest thread running through the module is the tension between technical performance and professional accountability, and this sharpened considerably between the Unit 6 group project and the Unit 11–12 individual project. In Unit 6, my central challenge was justifying why the 95th percentile of reconstruction error was an appropriate anomaly threshold rather than an arbitrary cut-off. Reflecting through Schön's (1983) distinction between reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action, I recognised that during the build I made rapid, largely intuitive decisions, but it was only in retrospective reflection that I properly connected that threshold to pricing insight and to the consequences of false positives and false negatives. Tutor feedback was direct: it identified gaps in my critical justification and pushed me to move beyond descriptive reporting toward analytical reasoning about why the decision was defensible. This matches the University's own guidance that reflective analysis should occupy most of the word count, since description alone risks the kind of writing that fails to connect theory with personal experience (University of Portsmouth, 2015, cited in University of Essex Online, no date). Acting on that feedback, I began treating every subsequent modelling decision as requiring an explicit, evidenced rationale.


My contribution to Unit 6 went beyond modelling: coordinating deliverables strengthened my ability to work within a virtual professional team, negotiating scope and responsibility with teammates who had different confidence levels with unsupervised learning, an experience that made my own assumptions about task ownership more evident to me (Hilzensauer, 2008). I also contributed critically to peers' work in Units 2, 4 and 10, evaluating the CrowdStrike outage and generative AI governance discussions. This pushed me to examine cybersecurity resilience, human oversight and context-aware deployment, and articulating why a peer's argument was under-evidenced forced me to be more precise about my own standards of evidence.


By Units 11–12, my approach to justification had matured considerably. Rather than choosing a dataset that fit an idealised recommender-system template, I deliberately selected the Spotify Tracks dataset knowing it lacked user level interactions, and reframed the task as content based ranking with popularity as a proxy relevance score. This limitation became part of the critical analysis itself, allowing me to investigate popularity bias and the risk of reinforcing already-successful artists. Training a HistGradientBoostingRegressor against a Multilayer Perceptron, validated with GroupKFold split by primary artist rather than a random split, tested generalisation to unseen artists rather than memorisation. The neural network's RMSE was only marginally higher than the classical model's, an important finding in itself: added complexity did not meaningfully improve the outcome. Recommending the classical model despite that near-parity required weighing performance against interpretability, computational cost and fairness across genre, explicit content and artist activity, rather than defaulting to the lowest-error option. This is precisely the evaluative judgement Unit 6's feedback had pushed me toward, and I could trace a direct line from that earlier correction to this more confident analysis. Applying SHAP explainability and subgroup diagnostics further reinforced that average performance metrics can mask harm to specific subpopulations, a professional and ethical issue as much as a technical one.

Evaluation

What worked well was my growing willingness to treat feedback as a driver of rigour rather than a verdict on quality, the gap identified in Unit 6 directly informed the explicit deployment trade-offs in Units 11–12. More challenging was maintaining that same rigour in peer feedback, where I had to balance honesty with professional courtesy. Personally, the module extended my confidence in unsupervised and neural methods; professionally, it strengthened my ability to communicate technical trade-offs to non-technical audiences.

Action Plan and Future Growth

I intend to deepen my understanding of explainable AI beyond SHAP, build subgroup fairness checks into every project by default, and extend the Spotify project with genuine user interaction data where available. I will also formalise collaborative practice, documenting individual contribution from the outset, and continue applying the evidenced, justification-driven discipline this module has instilled.
